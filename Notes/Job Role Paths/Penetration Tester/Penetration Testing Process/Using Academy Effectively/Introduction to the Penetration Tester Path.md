# Introduction to the Penetration Tester Path

These notes introduce you to the Penetration Tester Job Role Path and give you a high-level view of what penetration testing actually involves. You should treat this module as your anchor point. It is worth coming back to it regularly as you move through the rest of the path, as it helps you understand how each topic fits into the wider penetration testing workflow 

If you are new to penetration testing, or even new to HTB Academy, this is where you should begin. The aim is to give you enough context so that later modules do not feel disconnected or purely tool-driven.

This path is designed for you whether you are just starting out or already have experience and want to strengthen weaker areas or gain a different perspective. As you work through it, you will cover the core knowledge required for external penetration tests, internal network and Active Directory assessments, and web application security testing. Each module builds on the last and focuses on techniques, methodology, and tooling that you are likely to encounter in real assessments.

You will move through all stages of a penetration test, starting with reconnaissance and enumeration and finishing with documentation and reporting. The learning is deliberately hands-on, and the goal is to help you think and operate like a penetration tester rather than simply follow step-by-step instructions. By the end of the path, you should be capable of approaching basic to intermediate real-world environments with confidence and structure.

## HTB Academy Learning Philosophy

The core idea behind this path is to help you understand both how systems are built and how they fail. You are encouraged to develop your own repeatable methodology that you can apply to any engagement, regardless of the client’s size or industry.

As you work through the modules, you will complete many practical exercises and assessments. This repetition is intentional. By practising the fundamentals over and over again, you build strong habits around tasks that appear in almost every penetration test. When you are comfortable with the basics, you free up time and mental capacity to explore deeper issues and provide more value during an engagement.

For every vulnerability or misconfiguration you encounter, the focus is not just on exploitation. You are guided to understand the root cause, why a tool behaves the way it does, and how the issue could be remediated. This approach helps you give clearer, more accurate advice that can be adapted to a client’s specific environment and risk tolerance.

At a high level, the philosophy can be summarised as learning by doing, following a risk-based approach, and always operating within legal and ethical boundaries. The aim is to help you understand how to discover, exploit, explain, remediate, and even prevent issues, so you can grow into a well-rounded professional.

## Ethical and Legal Considerations

As you study penetration testing, you must constantly keep legality and ethics in mind. During an authorised assessment, you are permitted to perform actions that would otherwise be illegal. That permission only exists within a clearly defined scope and time window.

Throughout this path, you are given controlled labs and environments where you can practise safely and legally. These environments exist so you can learn without putting yourself or others at risk. You should never test systems that you do not own or have explicit written permission to assess.

Passive activities such as open-source intelligence gathering using public sources are generally acceptable for practice. However, any form of scanning or interaction with live systems without written authorisation is illegal and can lead to serious consequences. Always ensure that a formal scope of work, rules of engagement, and contract are in place before any testing begins.

If you want additional real-world experience, bug bounty programmes can be a good option, provided you strictly follow each programme’s scope and rules. Many of these programmes prohibit automated scanning, which makes them a useful way for you to practise manual testing and careful information gathering.

Once you begin working professionally, it is your responsibility to confirm that every engagement is legitimate. Ensure you have access to signed documentation that clearly defines what is in scope, including hosts, networks, applications, or any social engineering targets. If you notice something interesting that is not listed in scope, do not test it. Ask first, and make sure any changes are approved in writing.

Clients place a great deal of trust in you. The tools you use can cause outages or data loss if misused. You should always work with a “do no harm” mindset and think carefully before running exploits or proof-of-concept code. If there is any doubt, escalate the decision and obtain explicit approval before proceeding.

When in doubt, document everything and communicate clearly. Taking care of compliance and administrative details allows you to focus on delivering high-quality assessments and building long-term professional relationships.

## Next Steps

After completing this path, you should consider working towards a specialisation, such as Active Directory, web applications, or reverse engineering, while still maintaining a broad skill set. Depth in one area combined with solid general knowledge will make you more effective overall.

If you feel underprepared at any point, it is worth revisiting foundational material before continuing. Taking the modules in order is strongly recommended, as concepts are intentionally revisited and expanded over time. Skills such as lateral movement and post-exploitation are not isolated phases but iterative processes that appear throughout an engagement, and you will see them reinforced repeatedly as you progress.

Use these notes as a living reference. Update them as your understanding improves, and treat them as a record of how you are building your own penetration testing methodology.
