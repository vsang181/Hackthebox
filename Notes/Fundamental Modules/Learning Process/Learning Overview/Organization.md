# Organization

You have already seen the idea of overview appear several times, and there is a good reason for that. To understand why it matters so much, imagine the following situation.

You are standing on top of a mountain. Below you lies a vast forest. In the distance, on the horizon, there is another mountain that you want to reach. Once you descend into the forest, you will no longer be able to see your destination. From that point on, the only way forward is by orienting yourself using landmarks such as rivers, lakes, and clearings.

To make this journey possible, you would prepare properly. You would bring the tools you need, set intermediate reference points, and keep track of where you are going. Without this preparation, getting lost is almost guaranteed. Once you lose orientation, moving forward blindly rarely helps. In most cases, you would need to backtrack to regain your bearings before continuing.

This is exactly how learning and penetration testing work.

All of the concepts you have worked through so far are like the first markings on your map. They help you understand where you currently are and where you are trying to go. Organisation gives you context and direction. Without it, even good technical knowledge becomes difficult to use effectively.

In penetration testing, organisation is especially important because your work does not end with exploitation. Everything must eventually be turned into structured documentation and reporting. If you are disorganised during testing, writing a clear report later becomes far more difficult.

Time also plays a role. You may compromise multiple systems in a single day. If your notes are scattered or incomplete, you will waste time repeatedly searching for information you have already discovered.

A simple example illustrates this well.

An inexperienced woodcutter spends a short time sharpening their axe and hours cutting down a tree.
An experienced woodcutter spends most of their time sharpening the axe and only a short time cutting.

The result is the same, but the efficiency is completely different.

Organisation works the same way. Time spent structuring your work, notes, and process upfront saves you far more time later.

There are many ways to stay organised, and no single method is correct for everyone. Some common approaches include:

- Scrum-style task tracking
- Agile workflows
- Simple to-do lists
- Bullet journals

The specific method matters less than consistency. Choose a system that works for you, stick to it, and refine it over time. Organisation is not about rigid rules; it is about creating clarity so you can focus your energy where it matters most.
