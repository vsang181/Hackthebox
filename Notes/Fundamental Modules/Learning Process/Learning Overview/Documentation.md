# Documentation

When you document your work, the first thing you should decide is **who the documentation is for**. You will write very different notes for yourself than you would for a client or stakeholder. Internal notes focus on helping you reproduce steps and understand your own thinking, while client-facing documentation focuses on communicating findings and impact clearly.

At its core, documentation exists to make information understandable and reproducible. Someone reading it should be able to follow what you did, why you did it, and how to repeat the same activity if needed.

For that reason, good documentation always has three key qualities:

- **Overview** – the reader should quickly understand what the documentation is about
- **Structure** – information should be logically organised and easy to navigate
- **Clarity** – steps, results, and conclusions should be unambiguous

As you learn and practise, you will collect large amounts of information from many different sources. Without a structured way of recording this, important details are easily lost or forgotten.

## Tools and Visual Aids

Using a dedicated note-taking tool can make a big difference. A structured documentation tool allows you to organise notes hierarchically and link related concepts together, which is especially useful when revisiting material later.

Visuals are just as important. A clear screenshot can often explain something faster and more accurately than a long paragraph of text. Being able to capture and annotate screenshots directly saves time and reduces confusion.

Remember: clarity matters more than volume. Adding screenshots, diagrams, or short explanations at the right point is far more effective than dumping raw output or long blocks of text.

## Writing with the Reader in Mind

Regardless of who will read your documentation, a few principles always apply:

- Put yourself in the reader’s position and ask what they need to understand
- Avoid unnecessary repetition and vague wording
- Keep sentences simple and focused
- Make it easy to scan and follow, not something that feels like hard work to read

Before producing documentation for a client, it helps to clarify what matters most to them. Some readers care about technical detail, others care about risk, impact, and remediation. Tailoring the level of detail to the audience makes your documentation far more effective.

Good documentation is not about writing more. It is about writing **just enough**, in the right way, so that the information remains useful long after the work itself is finished.
