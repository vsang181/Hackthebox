## Way Of Learning

Let us take a maths exercise as an example:

```
20 * ________+ ________ = 65535
```

Think about how you approached this problem. You solved it the way you were taught to solve similar problems in the past. You relied on familiar patterns and methods that were already in your head. At that moment, you did not question whether there were other valid ways to reach the same result. You simply followed what felt “correct”.

This is exactly where the hacker mindset starts to matter.

Thinking outside the box means recognising that many of the limits you follow are not real constraints. They are habits. In penetration testing, you will constantly run into unfamiliar technologies, behaviours, and edge cases. If you only rely on what you already know and never pivot your thinking, you will quickly become frustrated or stuck.

Pivoting does not mean guessing randomly. It means deliberately changing your perspective when your current approach stops working.

## Learning, Emotions, and Frustration

A problem is not just a technical challenge. It is also an emotional experience. Without emotions, a problem is simply a situation.

Feelings such as confusion or frustration usually come from the way you are looking at the situation, not from the situation itself. This is important to understand because learning is not purely theoretical or practical. Your emotional state has a direct impact on how well you absorb information and how long you can stay focused.

When you feel confident and believe you will eventually reach your goal, you are far more likely to push through difficult topics. When you feel lost or directionless, even small obstacles can feel overwhelming.

## Why Goals Matter

To understand this better, imagine the following scenario.

You are standing in a room. An instructor tells you to start moving. You begin walking. After a short while, the instructor places a chair directly in your path. What happens next? You might stop and sit down on the chair.

Now change one detail.

The instructor tells you to walk to the opposite corner of the room. You start moving again. The chair appears in your path once more. This time, you walk around it and continue towards the corner.

The obstacle did not change. The goal did.

When you know where you are going, obstacles become something to work around rather than something that stops you completely. When you do not have a goal, the first obstacle often becomes the end of progress.

This applies directly to learning penetration testing. Without a clear objective, it is easy to drift between topics, tools, and techniques without building real understanding. With a goal, even confusion and frustration become part of the journey rather than reasons to quit.

As you continue learning, always be clear about what you are trying to achieve. The goal gives structure to your thinking, keeps your emotions in check, and allows you to keep moving forward, even when things stop making sense for a while.
