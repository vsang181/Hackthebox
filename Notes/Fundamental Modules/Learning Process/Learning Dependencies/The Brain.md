# The Brain

Before you move further, it helps to understand a little about how your brain works and why it matters for learning. You do not need deep knowledge of neuroscience, but having a basic mental model will make the next sections clearer and more practical 

Your brain is one of the most complex and least understood organs we know. It is also surrounded by many persistent myths. One common example is the idea that humans use only 5–10 percent of their brain. This is simply not true. Brain imaging studies using EEG and functional MRI have shown that activity occurs across all regions of the brain, even when you are resting.

Another widespread misconception is that learning speed is a measure of intelligence. Many people assume that if someone learns slowly, they are less capable. In reality, the brain regions responsible for complex reasoning and logic are not fully developed until roughly the early twenties, and even then, development varies significantly between individuals.

You can see this effect clearly in everyday life. Some people struggle in school but later excel in specific fields. Others perform exceptionally well in technical areas while finding social interaction difficult. These differences are linked to how specific brain regions develop and adapt over time. The brain changes physically as you learn, strengthening some pathways while leaving others less developed.

Even though we do not fully understand how the brain works, we do know that certain methods of learning and practice influence it in predictable ways. You will return to this idea repeatedly as you continue through the material.

## Thought and Information Processing

Despite decades of research, science still cannot definitively explain what a thought actually is. Cognitive science studies both conscious and subconscious processes such as memory, perception, language, problem-solving, and decision-making. What researchers do agree on is that thoughts are not physical objects, at least based on current evidence.

For practical purposes, it helps to think of a thought as a process rather than a thing.

One useful way to describe it is this: a thought is your brain’s response to one or more internal or external influences, where information is interpreted and connected to what you already know. These connections are shaped by your past experiences and habits.

Consider this example:

> Scotland has 421 different words for snow.

When you read that sentence, your brain immediately reacts. You interpret the information, relate it to what you already know about language or Scotland, and form internal links. Over time, these links create networks of associations. Your memory works by building and reusing these association chains.

This matters because learning is essentially the process of creating, strengthening, and reusing these connections.

## Conscious and Subconscious Thinking

Your brain is constantly active, producing thoughts even when you are not aware of them. Research has not conclusively shown whether the brain is more active when you are awake or asleep. What is clear is that thinking happens on both conscious and subconscious levels.

Conscious thinking is what you experience directly. When you are reading text on a screen, notice that you are reading it, and decide to look away, you are acting consciously. Consciousness allows you to observe both the external world and your internal state and to act intentionally.

Subconscious thinking works differently. You do not perceive subconscious thoughts directly. Instead, you experience them through emotions, instincts, or gut feelings. Your subconscious processes far more information than your conscious mind.

Experiments have shown that decisions are often initiated subconsciously before you become aware of them. One well-known set of experiments demonstrated that the brain can respond to stimuli without conscious awareness, yet still influence behaviour. In some cases, people correctly respond to stimuli they claim they did not consciously perceive.

This has an important implication for learning.

Much of what you absorb through repetition and practice is stored and processed subconsciously. That is why consistent hands-on work matters so much. Over time, patterns become familiar, and recognition happens automatically, without deliberate effort.

Understanding this helps explain why practice feels slow at first and then suddenly becomes easier. You are not just learning facts; you are reshaping how your brain processes information.

As you continue, keep this in mind. Learning is not only about effort at the conscious level. It is also about giving your brain enough structured exposure to build strong subconscious patterns that you can rely on later.
