# Decision Making

Decision-making is a complex topic with many theories and models attempting to explain how humans choose between options. In practice, it is difficult to define strict phases or rules because every person has unique thought patterns shaped by experience, emotion, and context 

At its simplest, a decision is the act of choosing one option from several available ones. You usually decide based on what you believe will bring the greatest benefit. Importantly, this process is never purely rational. Emotions play a significant role, often without you realising it.

Consider a simple situation. You unexpectedly get a day off work. A friend asks for help moving house, while you also have a personal project you want to finish to improve your chances of a salary increase. If supporting your friend matters more to you at that moment, you help them move. If financial progress feels more important, you work on your project. In both cases, the decision is influenced by values and emotions as much as logic.

Research in decision psychology shows that humans rarely behave according to strict cost–benefit calculations. Many rational decision models fail to reflect real human behaviour. Rationality is usually only one part of the process.

## The Trolley Problem

A well-known example used to explore decision-making is the trolley problem.

A train is moving towards five people on a track. You can pull a switch to divert the train onto another track where only one person is standing. Do you intervene and sacrifice one person to save five, or do you do nothing?

Before reading further, it is worth pausing and thinking about what you would do and why.

Large-scale studies involving tens of thousands of participants across many countries have explored how people respond to this scenario. Some researchers argue that the problem is too extreme and unrealistic. They point out that:

- the psychological burden of such a decision is enormous
- there may be no truly “correct” answer
- personal experience strongly shapes responses

What matters here is not the moral conclusion, but how the decision is framed.

## Hidden Constraints and False Choices

At first glance, the trolley problem appears to offer only two options:

- pull the switch and one person dies
- do nothing and five people die

This is very similar to the earlier maths exercise:

```
20 * ________ + ________ = 65535
```

You are presented with what looks like a limited set of choices, but those limits are assumed rather than stated.

In both cases, the result seems unavoidable: someone dies, or the equation must be solved in a specific way. However, these constraints are self-imposed.

What if you challenge them?

In the trolley scenario, you are never told that you cannot move, place objects on the track, warn the workers, or interfere with the train in other ways. Those limitations exist only because you accepted the framing of the problem without questioning it.

This is a critical lesson.

You often restrict your own decisions by assuming rules that were never defined.

## Asking Better Questions

Once you start questioning assumptions, new questions appear:

- How far away is the train?
- How fast is it moving?
- How much time do you have?
- What tools or options are available?
- Are the people on the track aware of the danger?

None of these details are provided, just as key information was missing in the maths example. Adding even one new factor can completely change the decision. If the workers are conscious, warning them could save everyone. If timing allows, more creative solutions may exist.

The more relevant information you gather, the better your decisions become.

## Implications for Learning and Penetration Testing

When someone claims that something is impossible, they are really saying that they cannot see a solution. They do not know all the factors involved. The danger is accepting that belief and applying it to yourself.

Many people give up before they even try because they assume there are only limited options. This leads to failure without effort.

If you feel stuck or unable to decide, the most common reason is a lack of information. Once you gather more facts, new paths appear, and better decisions become possible.

## Bringing It All Together

Your success becomes very difficult to question when you do the following:

- clearly decide on a goal
- genuinely want to achieve it
- understand how your brain processes information
- gather enough facts to make informed decisions

Decision-making improves when you stop accepting false limits, ask better questions, and align your choices with a clearly defined goal.
