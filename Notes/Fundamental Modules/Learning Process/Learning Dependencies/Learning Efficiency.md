## Learning Efficiency

One of the biggest challenges in information security is the sheer volume of material you are expected to deal with. As you saw earlier with the programming language example, there is an overwhelming number of topics, tools, and technologies. Most courses are highly technical, and that is a good thing. You need technical depth to understand how systems work, how they are structured, and how they fail. All the information required to succeed already exists.

The real difficulty lies elsewhere.

The hardest problem to solve is how to **combine what you already know, adapt to new environments, and absorb new information at the same time**.

Often, the issue is not that information does not exist. It is that you do not yet know what information you actually need. Before you can move forward, you must be able to answer two questions honestly:

- What do you already understand?
- What do you not understand yet?

Even when you find relevant material, it can still feel unusable if you lack an overall picture. Without context, isolated facts do not translate into practical ability.

Another major challenge is learning how to handle this volume of information and shape it around your own strengths and weaknesses rather than trying to memorise everything equally.

## Failure as a Learning Tool

Consider this example.

A student wants to learn how to assemble an engine. Before touching any tools, they might start reading theory to avoid making mistakes. That sounds reasonable, but it misses a critical point.

Failure is unavoidable, and it is essential.

Experience is built through mistakes. When something does not work as expected, you learn how to respond, adjust, and recover. That experience becomes invaluable later when things go wrong in unfamiliar environments.

A more effective approach is guided practice. Imagine assembling the engine alongside an instructor. You learn what you need at the moment you need it. You make mistakes in a controlled setting, see what can go wrong, and gradually build confidence. Once you can assemble the engine with guidance, you are in a much better position to deepen your theoretical understanding through repetition and refinement.

This is the philosophy behind the Academy structure.

## What You Are Really Learning

Through this approach, you are not only learning the fundamentals of penetration testing. You are also training yourself to:

- Learn faster
- Structure your knowledge
- Find relevant information efficiently
- Maintain a clear overview

These skills matter just as much as technical knowledge.

Many organisations are looking for penetration testers and security professionals. The question they are really asking is not whether you know a specific tool, but whether you know what you are doing.

Being “good” means you can act with intention. That only comes from experience. Experience builds a mental repertoire: patterns, associations, and reference points you can draw from when solving problems.

## How Much Practice Is Enough?

You may have heard of the so-called “10,000-hour rule,” which suggests that mastery requires an enormous time investment. Spending that long on a single skill is not realistic for most people.

If you look deeper, you will find alternative perspectives. Josh Kaufman, for example, argues that you can become competent at a new skill in around 20 hours of focused practice. Even 45 minutes a day can produce meaningful results if used deliberately.

This idea aligns well with the Pareto Principle, also known as the 80/20 rule. In many areas, roughly 20 percent of the effort produces 80 percent of the results. Chasing the final 20 percent of perfection often requires disproportionate effort and time.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is effectiveness.

All of these ideas tie together through association. By combining focused practice, clear goals, and both active and passive learning, you can progress far more efficiently than by trying to learn everything at once.

Understanding the learning curve, and how to move along it intentionally, is what allows you to improve quickly and sustainably in this field.
