# The Will

The concept of will is interpreted differently across many disciplines, but for your purposes, it can be understood in a practical way. In psychology, will is closely tied to conscious decision-making and intentional action. It is the moment where you decide to act rather than react.

In broader terms, will covers several related ideas: choosing a goal, committing to it, and translating that decision into deliberate action. From a philosophical perspective, will is the act of selecting a course of action based on conscious motives. Put simply, it is the effort you make to move towards a goal you have chosen.

For learning and self-improvement, will only becomes meaningful once you have decided what you want to achieve. Without that decision, there is nothing to sustain effort when things become difficult.

## Desire and Direction

Before committing to a goal, you need clarity around your desire. You should be able to explain to yourself what you want to achieve and how it would feel to reach that point. This often starts as imagination or daydreaming, where you picture yourself in a situation you would like to be in.

Many people abandon this process early because they cannot see a clear path forward. What they miss is that the path itself is not something you can fully plan in advance. The path is created by the steps you take, one after another.

If you look at interviews with successful actors, engineers, or scientists, you will rarely find someone who followed a perfectly planned career. Most opportunities appeared as a result of movement, not prediction.

## Belief and Fear

Desire is closely connected to belief. When you believe in your ability to learn and adapt, mental barriers begin to weaken. This allows you to access ideas and associations that fear would otherwise block.

It is worth internalising this idea:

Fear is a state created by imagining future outcomes so intensely that the present moment is ignored.

Fear has a purpose in genuinely dangerous situations. It keeps you alive. However, fear that appears while sitting in front of a computer, worried about whether you are “good enough”, is usually irrational. At that point, you are reacting to an imagined future rather than reality.

Ask yourself a few simple questions:

- Have you already worked through all the material?
- Have you fully seen how the material is taught?
- Are you realistically expected to already have these skills?

If the answer is no, then the fear you are feeling is not justified. You are judging yourself against expectations that do not yet apply.

## Fear, Avoidance, and Practice

In penetration testing, this fear often shows up in a paradoxical way. Some learners believe they are afraid of failing, but when they encounter a difficult topic, they stop trying. In doing so, they create the very failure they wanted to avoid.

Giving up feels easier than staying with discomfort. However, improvement only happens through continued practice and experimentation. Struggling with a topic is not failure; it is part of the process.

This is why keeping a clear goal matters so much. A defined objective gives you something to return to when motivation drops or fear appears. It reminds you why you started and helps you push through moments where progress feels slow.

Will is not about forcing yourself endlessly. It is about choosing a direction, committing to it, and continuing to take small steps forward, even when the path is unclear.
