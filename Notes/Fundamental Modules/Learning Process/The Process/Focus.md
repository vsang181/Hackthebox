# Focus

Let us go one level deeper and talk about **focus**. Focus is a critical skill, but like many powerful abilities, it is a double-edged sword. When you focus, you deliberately narrow your thinking and attention onto a single subject for a period of time. Everything else temporarily fades into the background.

This can be extremely effective, but it also explains some behaviours that may seem odd at first.

Have you noticed how many people go straight to the gym after a stressful day instead of resting? They often say they need it to calm down. On the surface, this sounds illogical. They are already exhausted, yet they choose to spend more energy.

So why does additional physical effort help?

Part of the explanation lies in biology. Physical exercise releases endorphins, which reduce pain, improve mood, support immune function, regulate hunger, and promote better sleep. That explains why the body feels better afterwards.

However, the most significant stress is usually not physical. It is mental.

During exercise, your attention is fully absorbed by the movement, breathing, and physical effort. That intense focus pulls your mind away from the thoughts that were causing stress. While your conscious focus is elsewhere, your subconscious continues working in the background, processing unresolved thoughts and emotions. In many cases, this leads to a sense of relief or clarity after the workout.

You have likely experienced something similar in everyday life.

Have you ever struggled to remember where you placed something or recall a word that felt obvious, only for it to suddenly come back to you later?

This happens because you stopped forcing the problem. By shifting your focus, you allowed your subconscious to work on it without pressure.

## Focus vs Attention

It is important to distinguish between **focus** and **attention**, because they are not the same thing.

Attention refers to what is happening right now. At this moment, your attention is on reading this text.
Focus refers to the goal or subject your thinking is aligned with.

Consider the example of misplaced keys. If your thoughts are:

“Where did I put my keys?”
“When did I last have them?”

Your focus is clearly on finding the keys.

Now imagine you are in a hurry. You keep checking the time, thinking about where you are supposed to be, and worrying about being late. Even if your attention is still scanning the room, your focus has shifted. You are no longer focused on finding the keys but on being late. As a result, your effectiveness drops.

This shows that attention is influenced by focus, not the other way around.

## Focus and Will

Focus is guided by your will and by what you want to achieve. Sometimes this alignment is conscious, and sometimes it happens subconsciously due to external pressure or internal emotion.

True focus is a deliberate alignment towards a specific goal.

When you are focused, persistence increases. Distractions lose their power. Setbacks feel smaller. If you know your goal, it becomes much easier to align your focus with it.

This alignment improves efficiency, reduces frustration, and helps you move forward faster. Instead of reacting to every external influence, you stay oriented towards what actually matters.

Focus is not about forcing yourself endlessly. It is about choosing a direction and repeatedly bringing your attention back to it when it drifts. Over time, this becomes one of the most valuable skills you can develop.
