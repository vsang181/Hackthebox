# Handling Frustration

Frustration is an emotional response to events or situations where expectations are not met. It often shows up as disappointment, helplessness, or the feeling of being stuck. The intensity varies from person to person and depends largely on what you expected to happen versus what actually happened.

You will usually encounter frustration in two forms:

- **External frustration**, caused by outside influences such as criticism, pressure, or negative feedback
- **Internal frustration**, driven by your own conscious or subconscious thought patterns

An important point to understand is that emotions are signals of underlying thought processes. Even when you are not fully aware of what you are thinking, your feelings often reveal it. One effective technique is to mentally step back and listen to your thoughts as if they were spoken by someone else, or imagine a close friend saying them out loud. This distance makes it easier for you to assess the situation objectively rather than emotionally.

## Frustration Tolerance

Everyone has a different level of frustration tolerance. If your tolerance is low, unexpected resistance or delayed success can quickly lead to giving up. This often results in increased stress, avoidance behaviour, or even aggressive reactions.

The good news is that frustration tolerance is **trainable**.

You have probably seen people who remain calm in situations that would overwhelm others. The situation itself is not necessarily easier for them; they simply react differently to it. Their tolerance allows them to stay composed while others become emotionally overloaded.

To understand why frustration appears, it helps to look at it structurally.

Frustration often occurs when you lack a required resource. In penetration testing and information security, this resource is very frequently information. You may already have encountered the phrase “enumeration is key”. When information is missing, frustration increases.

Your reaction to this lack of information depends heavily on your skill level. If you do not yet have the skills to obtain the information yourself, frustration can quickly turn into anxiety. This links directly back to comfort zones: unfamiliar territory feels uncomfortable until experience reduces uncertainty.

Understanding how these concepts connect helps you return to a more stable frustration tolerance level.

## Training Frustration Tolerance

To build tolerance, you need to place yourself in frustrating situations **deliberately and consciously**. This is crucial. The situation must be entered by choice, not forced upon you unexpectedly.

Consider the following example and pay attention to how it feels.

Imagine you need to catch a train and, due to circumstances beyond your control, you are forced to run several miles to reach it. You are stressed, exhausted, and possibly still miss the train. The frustration in this scenario is usually high, and blame is often directed outward.

Now change one detail.

You _knowingly_ leave the house late and decide to run to catch the train anyway. Even if you miss it, the frustration is usually much lower. You are more likely to analyse your own decisions instead of reacting emotionally to external factors.

The situation did not change. Your **decision** did.

This is a powerful insight.

## Frustration Is Temporary

Frustration feels intense, which is why many people panic when it appears. Some even react aggressively because they believe the feeling will last. It will not.

Frustration is temporary. It always passes.

What remains is the experience you gained while dealing with it.

Over time, repeated exposure to manageable frustration makes you calmer and more confident. You learn that you can tolerate discomfort without losing control. This strengthens self-confidence and improves decision-making under pressure.

You can influence and regulate **internal frustration** through awareness and deliberate action. External frustration, caused by factors outside your control, is harder to manage directly. However, even there, your response remains within your control.

Frustration is not a signal to stop. It is a signal that you are learning something new.
