# Questioning

Learning how to ask the right questions is a critical skill and, in many ways, an art. This applies whether you are dealing with technical problems or everyday situations. In practice, many people do not struggle with answers but with questions themselves. Most people are not even fully aware of what a question actually represents.

At first glance, it is tempting to define questions purely as a way to gather information and facts so that we can draw conclusions and make decisions. That view is incomplete. Questions also serve as **orientation tools**. They help you understand where you are, what you are dealing with, and where your next step could be. In that sense, questions act like markers on a map before you move forward.

In cyber security, and especially in penetration testing, one principle is worth remembering:

**The hardest and most important part is not finding the right answer, but finding the right question.
**
If the answer to a problem is already known, the problem usually stops being difficult. What makes situations challenging is not a lack of answers, but uncertainty about where to start and what to ask.

You have likely experienced moments where you felt stuck and did not even know how to approach the problem. That feeling usually comes from poorly defined or missing questions.

Before continuing, it is worth doing a short exercise. Pick three to five difficult situations from your own life. For each one, write down a single question you asked yourself at the time. These do not have to be technical problems. Later, you will see how different questions would have changed the outcome.

## Question States

Before going further, one common myth needs to be removed:

**There are no “good” or “bad” questions.**

Those labels do not affect the answer at all. If a question produces the answer X, Y, and Z, then calling the question “good” or “bad” changes nothing. The result stays the same.

Calling a question “good” or “bad” usually reflects how useful the answer feels to the person asking it, not the quality of the question itself. The judgement belongs to the outcome, not to the question.

A more useful distinction is this:

- Rough questions
- Precise questions

For example:

- Rough: “How can I hack X?”
- Precise: “How can I use the server’s SMB service to identify existing user accounts?”

Precision influences the usefulness of the answer, but even a precise question is not “good” or “bad”. Those labels are irrelevant.

## Questions in Everyday Thinking

You ask questions constantly, often without noticing. On average, people ask several questions per minute internally.

You can test this. Set a one-minute timer. Every time you notice yourself asking a question in your thoughts, make a mark on paper. Most people are surprised by how many questions appear in such a short time.

Questions are a fundamental part of thinking. They create links between pieces of information in your brain. If you stop questioning while learning, your understanding drops sharply. Reading without questioning is like following a recipe that never explains how to cook the dish.

A recipe answers one core question:

### How do I prepare this dish?

The ingredients are the raw information. The method is the process. In learning, the content is equivalent to ingredients, and your questions define the method. Simply copying someone else’s approach rarely produces the same result, especially without experience.

The more questions you ask, the clearer the overall picture becomes.

## Goals and Question Direction

Once you have a goal and the willingness to reach it, questioning becomes more structured. Your questions begin to support decision-making, skill development, and obstacle handling.

Questions are often oriented around three aspects:

- origin
- process
- result or goal

They can relate to time, cause, effect, location, action, or interaction. Almost every question is shaped by your needs, context, and objective.

At this point, two deeper questions appear:

- What is a question, really?
- What purpose does it serve?

Official definitions usually describe a question as a sentence designed to elicit information. This definition breaks down quickly. A single word such as “Why?”, “How?”, or “Where?” is a valid question, yet it does not meet the formal definition of a sentence.

More importantly, a question does not automatically produce information. For example:

“How is Host A connected to Host B?”

Asking this question does not give you information. It only points you towards what you need to investigate. This shows that the true purpose of a question is not information itself, but **understanding relationships**.

## The Relationship-Oriented-Questioning (ROQ) Model

All questions share a common property: they describe relationships between components. Based on this idea, the Relationship-Oriented-Questioning (ROQ) model was created 

The model consists of five components:

- Your position – your perspective and context
- The object – the core subject of the question
- Known – information you already have
- Unknown – information you are missing
- Other positions – perspectives of others, if relevant

Every question must include at least two components. One of them is always your position, because you are the one asking the question.

Once the components are identified, you describe how they relate to each other:

- Connection – how components are linked
- Affection – how one component influences another

By mapping these relationships, you expose exactly what you know, what you do not know, and what needs investigation next.

## Why This Matters

The strength of this model is that it shows you where the gap is. Unknown elements become clear targets. When unknown becomes known, the model updates and stacks naturally.

If a question cannot be mapped using this model, it usually means the question is too vague and needs refinement. The model forces precision and prevents questions that lead nowhere.

## The Right Question

So what is the right question?

**A right question is a precise one that allows you to identify relationships, understand them, and move one step closer to the answer you need.
**
This is not about clever wording. It is about structure, clarity, and direction. Once you learn to question this way, problem-solving becomes faster, more deliberate, and far less frustrating.
