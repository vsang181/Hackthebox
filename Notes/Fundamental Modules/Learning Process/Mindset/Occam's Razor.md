# Occam's Razor

Thinking outside the box allows you to step beyond assumptions you did not even realise you were making. The downside is that once those mental boundaries disappear, you can suddenly see too many possible explanations. Problems can become unnecessarily complex very quickly. This is where **Occam’s Razor** becomes useful as a balancing principle 

Occam’s Razor is a long-standing idea used in science and problem-solving. In simple terms, it states that when multiple explanations are possible and all adequately explain the situation, the **simplest explanation is usually the correct one**.

In other words, avoid overcomplicating things when a straightforward answer is available.

## A Practical Example

Imagine your computer does not turn on. Immediately, you might start listing potential causes:

- Faulty power supply
- CPU failure
- Damaged motherboard

This approach often leads people to jump straight into elimination mode, tearing systems apart and checking internal components. While this feels logical, it ignores one key question:

### Why is the computer not receiving power at all?

Once you ask that question, your thinking naturally shifts. Power is not just about internal components. The chain starts much earlier: the wall socket, the extension lead, the power switch. If you widen your perspective and check those first, you might quickly realise the multi-plug is switched off.

In this case, the simplest explanation was also the correct one.

## Applying Occam’s Razor Correctly

Using Occam’s Razor sounds easy, but applying it well takes practice. The simplest explanation is often the most likely, but not always. You must learn to distinguish between:

- Individual steps and technical details
- The overall concept or system behaviour

This distinction becomes critical as you learn new topics.

For example, when you first encounter SQL injection, the individual detection and exploitation steps may feel complex and confusing. However, once you understand the core concept—unsanitised input influencing database queries—you will start recognising vulnerable patterns much more easily. The steps may change, but the underlying idea remains the same.

This is why focusing on concepts matters more than memorising procedures.

## Occam’s Razor in Penetration Testing

Penetration testing is often discussed in terms of techniques, tools, and step-by-step methods. While these are important, every real engagement is different. Even when organisations use the same software, their configurations, environments, and constraints are almost always unique.

If you apply Occam’s Razor here, the simplest and most reliable approach becomes clear:

You work with the information you can obtain.

How you gather and use that information may vary, but the core idea stays consistent. Techniques are interchangeable; understanding is not. If you only memorise steps, you will struggle when something does not behave as expected. If you understand the bigger picture, adapting becomes much easier.

This pattern repeats throughout learning and real assessments. Once you know the solution, everything looks obvious in hindsight. The steps suddenly seem simple. The real challenge is not capturing a flag or exploiting a vulnerability—it is **finding the path that leads there**.
