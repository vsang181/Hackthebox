# Way of Thinking

Information security is a huge field. You will never be able to learn everything, and trying to do so is not only unrealistic but counterproductive. What matters is how you think, how you learn, and how you adapt when you encounter something unfamiliar.

Let us look at a simple example to put this into perspective.

Imagine you decide to become a programmer. There are well over 200 programming languages in use today. If you spent just 100 hours learning each language, you would need around 20,000 hours in total. That works out to roughly seven years of full-time study, assuming eight hours a day. After all that time, you would have learned the languages, but you would not yet have practised debugging, reverse engineering, or analysing real programs. If you then decided to focus on those skills as well, you could easily spend another seven years.

Clearly, this approach does not make sense.

The reality is that you do not need to learn everything. You need to understand **core principles**. Once you understand how one programming language works at a deeper level, learning others becomes significantly easier. You also do not need to know every language in order to read or analyse code. Most languages follow the same underlying ideas, such as abstraction, structure, and data handling.

The same logic applies to information security. Your goal is not to memorise every tool, exploit, or technology. Your goal is to quickly understand how systems are built, how they are supposed to behave, and how they can fail. You will frequently find yourself in situations where you do not understand how something works. That is normal, and it is actually a good sign. It tells you exactly where your learning needs to focus next.

## Learning Mindset and Communities

There are many learning-focused security communities available, and they can be extremely valuable. You will find write-ups, vulnerable machines, guides, and discussions that help you improve your skills. As you interact with others, you will start to notice two broad groups of people:

- Those who genuinely know very little
- Those who think they know very little

This can be frustrating at times, but it is a normal part of the learning process. You should always communicate respectfully and remember that everyone in this field started with no knowledge at all. A healthy learning mindset and respectful discussion are critical, both for your own growth and for the community as a whole.

## Understanding Your Skill Level

One of the hardest things in penetration testing is accurately understanding your own level of knowledge. This field covers an enormous range of technologies, and it is easy to feel overwhelmed.

You can choose to learn a little bit about everything and master nothing, or you can specialise deeply in one area and ignore the rest. A more sustainable approach is to focus on **how you research**, **how you learn**, and **how you adapt**. If you know how to find the right information quickly and apply it to the environment you are working in, you will be effective even when you are dealing with unfamiliar systems.

However, this is not automatic. You must practise learning itself as a skill.

## Practice Over Theory

You do not become a good penetration tester by reading alone. Practical ability only comes through repeated, hands-on practice.

For example, you can read dozens of books about programming and understand how code works. This is passive learning, and it has value. But if you want to actually write software, you must actively write code, break it, fix it, and test it yourself. The same applies to security testing. Tools and techniques only become useful when you have used them repeatedly in real scenarios.

## When Are You “Good Enough”?

A common question is: When is a penetration tester good enough?

The answer is not tied to knowing everything. No one does. What matters is whether you can identify what you do not know, find reliable information, and adapt it to the situation in front of you.

At this point, three ideas usually come up:

- Finding information
- Choosing what is relevant
- Adapting it to the environment

There is one crucial concept missing from that list.

## Learning

Learning how to learn is difficult, and most people have never been taught how to do it properly. In traditional education, you are often shown a single way to solve a problem and then asked to repeat it. That approach does not translate well to penetration testing, where there is rarely a single correct path.

As you continue studying, your aim should be to move beyond copying steps and instead understand why something works. Once you can do that, you will be able to teach yourself new skills throughout your career, even as technologies and attack techniques continue to change.
