## Reporting Tips and Tricks

The biggest lesson is to build the report as you test, not after testing ends. That means filling in template sections early, writing findings as they are confirmed, and keeping the attack chain updated throughout the engagement.

***

## Work As You Go

If you wait until the end, reporting becomes rushed and errors creep in. A better approach is to prefill the fixed parts of the report early, then document the attack path, evidence, and findings in real time as you work. This reduces last-minute QA issues and makes the final report much easier to complete.

***

## Use Templates

You should not recreate every report from scratch. A clean template for each assessment type saves time and prevents embarrassing mistakes like leaving another client’s name in the document. Templates also help keep formatting and structure consistent across engagements.

***

## Word Features That Help

A few Word habits make reporting much easier:

- Use styles instead of direct formatting.
- Use table styles for consistency.
- Use built-in captions so figures renumber automatically.
- Add page numbers.
- Include a table of contents.
- Use bookmarks for jump points and section removal.
- Set up a custom dictionary for recurring technical terms.
- Apply language settings to code and terminal-output styles so spellcheck ignores them.
- Use custom numbering for findings and appendices.

***

## Useful Shortcuts

A few shortcuts and workflow tricks can save a lot of time:

- F4 repeats the last action.
- Ctrl+A then F9 updates fields like the table of contents.
- Ctrl+S should be used constantly.
- Ctrl+Alt+S splits the window so you can view two parts of the report at once.
- Shift+F5 jumps back to the last edit location.

***

## Automate Repetitive Work

If you build reports often, automation pays off quickly. Macros can help fill in client details, dates, scope, and testing type, and can also remove sections that do not apply to a given assessment. This is especially useful when maintaining one master template rather than many separate copies.

***

## Use a Findings Database

A findings database or sanitized finding library saves a huge amount of time. Many assessments repeat the same issues, so having prewritten content for common findings improves speed and consistency. It also reduces the chance of contradictory remediation guidance across different reports.

***

## Tell a Story

A strong report should read like a coherent story. Do not just list technical issues. Explain why the findings matter, how they chain together, and what business risk they create. That makes the report much more useful to both technical and non-technical readers.

***

## Show Enough Evidence

Evidence should be clear, relevant, and not excessive. Include enough screenshots and output to prove the issue and support reproduction, but avoid flooding the report with massive blocks of raw console text. Clean presentation matters just as much as technical accuracy.

***

## Redaction and Professionalism

Redact sensitive data whenever possible, including passwords, hashes, and other secrets. Do not rely on blur or pixelation for redaction. Use solid black blocks. Also remove anything unprofessional from screenshots and terminal output, and make sure browser captures do not show personal bookmarks or extensions.

***

## QA Matters

A report should go through at least one round of QA by someone other than the author whenever possible. If you are working alone, step away and review the report later with fresh eyes. A QA checklist in the template is also a good idea because it helps catch recurring mistakes before delivery.

***

## Client Communication

Keep the client informed from start to finish. Send a start notice at the beginning of the assessment and a stop notice at the end of each day. If you discover something critical, tell them promptly rather than waiting for the final report. Good communication builds trust and makes the eventual report review much smoother.

***

## Final Delivery

Once QA is complete, issue a draft report first, then review client feedback, then produce the final version. Some clients and auditors care deeply about the distinction between draft and final, so that versioning should be handled carefully. The goal is a clean, accurate deliverable that reflects the quality of the assessment and the professionalism of the tester.
